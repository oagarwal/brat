Welcome to the MD-EvidenceSearch Project

We are collecting “annotations”, or “mark up”, on articles describing the conduct and results of clinical trials ultimately to make finding relevant evidence easier. In particular, healthcare providers often want to know what treatment is likely to work best for their patient, according to the available evidence. But this is surprisingly hard to find! This is in part because results of clinical trials are published as articles meant for consumption by other humans, not machines.

The idea behind this project is to help machines by marking up the important bits of articles in a computer-friendly way, in turn enabling smarter search. For example, telling the computer that an article reports a trial in which a particular treatment (perhaps aspirin) was studied is helpful, because if someone is then specifically looking for evidence about aspirin as a treatment this article can immediately be retrieved. It is not always obvious from the article text alone that this is the case, even if the article contains the word “aspirin”.

To collect such mark-up, we are recruiting citizen scientists like yourself mark articles with respect to clinically important information. The big idea is depicted in the figure. Articles with your annotations attached will be stored in a database, which can then be used to improve search. Furthermore, these annotations are used to train “machine learning” systems, which learn to perform the mark-up automatically! Using this approach, we can automatically mark-up the articles that citizen scientists have not yet got to and deposit these into the database as well. The automated mark-up will probably not be as reliable as when real people do it, but it will likely still be good enough to improve search.



When you see an "Annotate button" next to an abstract, you can click on it to mark the abstract with important information. In each abstract, we are looking for the descriptions of Patients (medical problem, demographics), Interventions (how were the patients treated) and Outcomes (what measures of treatment success was used).

Please note that this is the starting version of the tool and may not be completely stable. Any feedback would be highly appreciated. Our contact information is available at the top of the page.
